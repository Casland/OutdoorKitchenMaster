# Project Context — Read This First

## The build in one paragraph

An L-shaped DIY outdoor kitchen (~9' × 11' legs) on an existing paver patio in San Diego, backed against a vinyl fence, next to a hot tub. Galvanized steel stud framing on a shimmed pressure-treated sill, cement board sheathing, wood-look porcelain plank cladding run vertically (weathered driftwood aesthetic), 18" tile backsplash, light leathered 3cm granite counter at 36" finished height. Appliances: Coyote SL gas grill (size TBD), Kamado Joe Big Joe in a corner cradle with a SMOBOT damper controller, a 24" front-venting fridge, and a cold-water bar sink. Budget landing zone: roughly $10,100–$15,000 all-in, appliances driving most of it.

## Site conditions that shaped everything

- **Existing paver patio** — no slab, no footing. This killed the masonry option (CMU requires a poured footing below grade) and made steel-stud-on-PT-sill the structural answer.
- **Vinyl fence directly behind** — already has scorch marks from previous grilling. Drives the 1.5–2" standoff air gap, the boarded back at the grill bay, and the "fence is a combustible" clearance checks.
- **Coastal San Diego** — salt air pushed the stud spec to 20-gauge with EQ coating; intense sun made countertop surface temperature the deciding material factor (dark stone hits 150–170°F); rare rain but the design still treats water seriously.
- **Utilities on site**: two electrical circuits already stubbed up near the fence (in the wrong spot — Phase 0 relocates them with offset fittings), and a cold water line already run. No sewer, no gas — both get deferred-tie-in provisions under the pavers.
- **Sewer constraint**: plumbers assessed the house cleanout as shallow relative to the run distance — the drain fall budget is TIGHT. This drove the "seat the drain pipe as high as possible, directly beneath the pavers" decision and the gravity → fence-run → pump fallback ladder.

## Owner profile

Capable, detail-obsessed DIYer. Built the full SketchUp model down to stud placement. Owns or will buy proper tools; rents the big stuff. Cost-conscious in the "bang for buck" sense, not the cheap sense — happily spends on durability, ruthlessly cuts specs that can't cite a source (personally caught and killed an over-spec'd double-cement-board + Rockwool grill bay). Wants a contractor-grade result and thinks in decade timescales ("what does this look like in year eight?"). Caught multiple real technical errors during planning (stud/track geometry, track-as-water-gutter problem) — treat them as a peer reviewer, not a novice.

## Design philosophy (recurring principles — the site should surface these)

1. **Never give water a place to sit.** Cabinet floors slope to weep holes; cladding terminates at a weep screed; bottom tracks get weep holes drilled in the back flange; the back of the island is open hardware cloth (a drying lung). Time-of-wetness, not wetting frequency, is what corrodes.
2. **One-way doors get checklists.** Anything unreachable after a step completes (panel backs before tilt-up, chases before the cradle blocks the corner, sleeves before pavers relay) is identified and front-loaded.
3. **Access forever.** Interior stays serviceable through door/drawer openings for life; the drain trap is above the cabinet floor; the fridge outlet is in the adjacent bay; a removable end panel serves the buried utilities; pre-installed chases cross the only zone that ever goes blind (the kamado corner).
4. **The manual is law.** Manufacturer install manuals (Coyote especially) override the guide on cutouts, ventilation areas, and clearances.
5. **One material discipline.** Wood-look porcelain planks (faces + doors), porcelain picket (backsplash), stone top: one wet saw rental, one thinset system, one grout strategy.
6. **Spend where physics demands, cut where nothing cites a source.** 20-ga studs, sill-seal isolation, cold-galv on every cut: non-negotiable. Double board + insulation at the grill bay: deleted when challenged, because non-combustible assembly (ASTM E136) is the whole requirement.
7. **Bank elevation.** With a tight drain fall budget, every design choice preserves height: pipe seated directly under pavers, 2" pipe over 3" (higher invert), trap arm exiting high (~18–20").

## How the design evolved (the arc, for the decision-log context)

1. **Framing material** (May): CMU/cinder block vs steel studs debated. Steel won on precision for appliance openings, utility routing through knockouts, and — decisively — cost: CMU needs a poured footing that pavers can't provide ($1,400–2,500 shell vs $650–1,250 steel; brick $2,900–5,200).
2. **Cooker lineup** (May): a freestanding pellet smoker (Camp Chef Woodwind Pro) was considered for integration; rejected — built-in pellet options are a thin market and a smoke-flavor downgrade, and counter-height mismatch makes freestanding integration ugly. A kamado in the corner won. The end wall is deliberately built as a clean butt-joint termination so the island can be EXTENDED later if the pellet smoker ever earns a bay.
3. **Kamado + controller** (May → June): Konnected Joe (built-in app/ignition) vs BGE XL + SMOBOT debated — "architecture over app": SMOBOT's motorized top damper has no fan, preserves natural draft, ~30% better charcoal life. Final: **Big Joe + SMOBOT (Kamado Joe version — the BGE daisy-wheel kit does NOT fit the Kontrol Tower) + Looftlighter** for ignition.
4. **Cladding** (June): costed stucco ($100–400, the SoCal-native choice), porcelain tile ($400–640), faux stone panels ($~2,000 — poor value at $12.50/sq ft with a bad 1.5-row cut problem at 36" height), cultured stone ($2,100–4,200). Then the owner's AI renders shifted the aesthetic to weathered driftwood WOOD. Real teak/ipe (silvers naturally) was considered eyes-open re: coastal UV/salt; final call: **wood-look porcelain plank, vertical** — the driftwood look with tile physics. Consequences absorbed: leveling clips + ≤33% offset (never 50% brick pattern — plank bow guarantees lippage), rectified tile, epoxy grout color-matched so joints read as reveals, metal edge trim at corners (never miter porcelain), and doors became aluminum-frame + GoBoard + plank with edges landing on grout joints.
5. **Countertop** (June): sun heat was the deciding factor. Dark granite and quartz (UV resin failure) rejected outright. Ranking: light leathered 3cm granite remnant ($1,000–1,800 fabricated) > large-format light porcelain tile (<$300 DIY) > Dekton. **Locked: light leathered 3cm granite.**
6. **The under-paver era** (July, final sessions): drain plan matured from "trench to sewer now" → Option C stub-out with a jug interim → actual 2" ABS laid under the seam with a long-sweep riser, seated high for the tight fall budget. Then a gas sleeve was added (straight 2" PVC chase; future CSST bends by hand at the exit; interior routing through stud-web knockouts; a pre-installed chase through the blind kamado corner), then a third universal spare chase. Trench order across the seam: drain, spare, gas — gas outermost.
7. **Back wall + fire assembly** (final sessions): hardware cloth across the entire back EXCEPT the grill bay, which is a fully boarded compartment (back pre-attached at tilt-up + both side partitions) — single layer, because the steel + cement assembly is already non-combustible and the double-board + Rockwool spec died under scrutiny. Kamado corner stays cloth (ceramic is a thermos; the boarded, porcelain-faced backsplash already shields the fence). Track weep holes added after the owner spotted the U-channel bathtub problem.

## Current status (as of July 2026)

Nothing built yet. Plan is complete and converged. First physical work: pull pavers at the seam, lay three pipes (drain / spare / gas chase), shoot laser, set PT sill. The grill-size decision (30" vs 36") gets made via cardboard mockup against the real sill, and it's the last thing gating the full stud cut list.

## Interim states the site should represent honestly

- Sink drains to a **sealed catch jug** until the sewer tie-in (a deliberate, legitimate bridge — with the honest note that the eventual tie-in should be permitted).
- Grill runs on **propane** (tank in the open under-grill bay, LP detector mounted LOW) until the gas tie-in; conversion to NG flips detection HIGH.
- Backsplash tile choice is late-binding by design; four AI-rendered comps exist (Coastal Slate Blue, Sea Glass Sage, Sand & Smoke pattern, Deep Ocean Mix). Sea Glass Sage is the standing recommendation next to the red kamado.
