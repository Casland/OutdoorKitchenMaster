# Decision Log

Each entry: what was decided, what it beat, and why. Status: 🔒 locked · ⚠️ open · 🔄 superseded (kept for history).

---

### D01 — Steel stud framing 🔒
**Beat:** CMU/cinder block, brick, wood studs, hybrid CMU+steel.
**Why:** (1) CMU cannot sit on pavers — it needs a poured footing ($400–700 + paver demo), which alone flips the cost comparison: steel shell $650–1,250 vs CMU $1,400–2,500 vs brick $2,900–5,200 in DIY materials. (2) The layout has many precision appliance openings; steel lets you move a stud half an inch with a new screw, block does not. (3) Utilities thread through stud knockouts vs core-drilling masonry. (4) Wood was never seriously in it: combustible framing triggers the grill's insulated-jacket requirement and rots. Steel + cement board is a non-combustible assembly by definition.

### D02 — 20-gauge EQ studs, 3-5/8", 10' sticks 🔒
**Beat:** 25-gauge interior studs.
**Why:** Coastal San Diego air + outdoor life; 25-ga is an interior drywall product. EQ label reads -18/-19/-20/-23 mil (avoid -14/-16). 10' sticks yield three ~33" studs each. Buy from supply yards (J&B El Cajon / Westside Sorrento Valley), not big-box; TEK screws online/Fastenal.

### D03 — PT 2×4 sill on shims, directly on pavers 🔒
**Beat:** adjustable pedestals under a PT sleeper; pedestals alone.
**Why:** Simpler, cheaper, anchors directly to pavers (Tapcons = the shear connection to earth). Shims pack UP to a laser datum from the pavers' high point — never grind down. Non-negotiables that ride with it: closed-cell sill seal between PT and steel (ACQ-treated lumber corrodes galvanized steel), G185/stainless for any fastener touching PT, PL Premium bedding.

### D04 — Stud length 32-13/16" 🔒
**Formula:** 34" − counter thickness. Counter locked at 3cm granite (1-3/16" actual). Stack: paver → shims → PT (1.5") → sill seal → track → stud → track → ½" deck board → granite = 36" finished. Dry-stack one test sandwich before gang-cutting fifty.

### D05 — Counter: light leathered 3cm granite remnant 🔒
**Beat:** dark granite, quartz, porcelain tile top, Dekton, poured concrete.
**Why:** Surface temperature in San Diego sun was the deciding factor — dark stone hits 150–170°F (unusable, and the owner has kids); quartz resin fails under UV (hard no outdoors). Light granite (Alaska White / Kashmir White class) stays usable, is impact-tough, and remnant-shops to $1,000–1,800 fabricated for ~40 sq ft. Porcelain tile was the strong budget runner-up (<$300 DIY, coolest surface, tile-level repairability). L needs two pieces, seam at the corner, away from the sink; seal twice a year.

### D06 — Cladding: wood-look porcelain plank, vertical 🔒
**Beat (in order of elimination):** faux stone panels ($~2,000, $12.50/sq ft, bad 1.5-row cut math at 36" height), cultured stone, natural stone veneer, stucco (cheapest and SoCal-native — eliminated only by aesthetics), real wood (teak/ipe/thermo-ash).
**Why:** The owner's renders converged on a weathered-driftwood wood aesthetic. Real wood silvering naturally was considered eyes-open, but coastal UV/salt maintenance lost to porcelain physics: the plank gives the exact look with tile durability. Install law: rectified tile, leveling clips (non-negotiable on planks), offset ≤33% — NEVER 50% brick pattern (plank bow guarantees lippage; manufacturers warn against it), back-butter everything, epoxy grout dead-matched to plank so joints read as reveals, Schluter-type metal trim at outside corners (never miter porcelain). Bonus that sealed the deal: the whole project collapsed to ONE material discipline — plank faces + picket backsplash + stone top = one wet saw rental, one thinset system, one grout strategy.

### D07 — Doors: aluminum frame + GoBoard + plank face 🔒 (hinge type ⚠️)
**Beat:** stainless doors (abandons the seamless look), minimizing doors in favor of drawers, cement-board-backed doors.
**Why:** Preserves "handles emerging from a continuous wood face." Bolt-and-bond frames (1×1 tube/angle, gussets, SS machine screws + DP420 structural adhesive — no welding). GoBoard over cement board because door WEIGHT kills hinges and foam board bonds porcelain equally well. Door edges land on grout joints so the reveal hides in a reveal. **Open:** owner wants Euro-style opening → clamp-on concealed hinges for aluminum frames preferred; stainless piano hinge is the sag-proof fallback. Decide at Phase 5; the Phase 1 hinge backing supports either.

### D08 — Drawers: DIY riveted aluminum boxes 🔒
**Beat:** pre-made stainless drawer units.
**Why:** Custom sizes required. .050–.063" sheet or 3–4mm ACP, 1×1 angle corners + stainless pop rivets, flat-bar rails at slide height, perforated bottoms (drainage), U-channel rim. Under 10 lb empty → standard stainless full-extension slides. Faces: tiled cement-board panels through-bolted with slotted holes for alignment.

### D09 — Kamado: Big Joe + SMOBOT (KJ version) + Looftlighter 🔒
**Beat:** Konnected Joe (built-in ignition/app), BGE XL + SMOBOT, BGE XL + Flame Boss 500, built-in pellet grills.
**Why:** "Architecture over app." SMOBOT is the only controller with no blower fan — a motorized top damper that preserves the kamado's natural draft and tested ~30% better charcoal life vs forced-air. Looftlighter covers the manual-ignition gap in ~10 minutes of active work. Big Joe won the body decision for cooking area. **Critical purchase note:** order the SMOBOT Kamado Joe version (~$349) — the standard BGE daisy-wheel kit does NOT fit the Kontrol Tower. Built-in requires 4 ceramic feet (airflow); dimension the cradle from the Ceramic Grill Store A–R chart; shelf sits below counter so band/latch stay reachable; assemble in place (never fits later).

### D10 — Pellet smoker: rejected from the island; extension-friendly end wall instead 🔒
**Why:** Built-in pellet market is thin and a smoke-flavor downgrade vs the owner's Woodwind Pro (dedicated smoke box); freestanding integration creates an ugly counter-height mismatch. The lasting design consequence: the island's end wall is a CLEAN BUTT-JOINT TERMINATION — no wrapped finish requiring demolition — so a future extension bay is a 15-minute planning decision that already happened.

### D11 — Grill: Coyote SL series ⚠️ (30" vs 36", LP vs NG)
**Cutouts:** SL30 25½"W / SL36 32½"W, both × 20½"D × 10¼"H, +~1" swap margin. **Deadline:** before framing the grill bay; everything else in the frame is identical. Method: cardboard mockup on the real sill. **Fuel:** ships fuel-specific. Gas tie-in a year+ out → order LP, budget the LP→NG conversion kit (~$60–100 + an hour of installer time). Tie-in imminent → order NG outright. Detection flips at conversion: propane sinks (detector low), NG rises (add high).

### D12 — Fridge: Velieta 24" front-vent built-in-capable 🔒
**Why:** Verified front-venting, compressor-based, adjustable feet — the three requirements for a built-in cavity. Honest framing: ~$350 is not Blaze-tier; the lined cavity gives it a sheltered life, and the standard 24" bay means any future replacement drops in. Frame the cavity to the actual cut sheet on arrival. Outlet in the ADJACENT bay (never behind the fridge), grommeted cord pass-through, sealed.

### D13 — Backsplash: 18" band, heat-zoned, no stainless 🔒 (tile choice ⚠️)
**Why:** Grout behind a grill is a grease trap → the ~4' grill zone gets smooth 24"+ light porcelain (the wipeable substitute for the stainless panel the owner ruled out). ~3' kamado corner smooth; ~11' feature tile. Epoxy grout one shade darker than instinct; silicone (never grout) at the counter joint and every material change; porcelain/glass only behind cook surfaces. **Open:** which of four rendered comps — Sea Glass Sage is the standing recommendation next to the red kamado; the SketchUp currently wears Coastal Slate Blue. Deadline: before counter templating.

### D14 — Back wall: hardware cloth everywhere EXCEPT the boarded grill bay 🔒
**History:** started as "cement board everywhere"; became "cloth everywhere" (ventilation + no rear access against the fence); finalized as zoned after two owner challenges.
**Why cloth wins generally:** open mesh cannot trap water; the back becomes the cavity's permanent drying lung; pest exclusion; installs from the front. Corrosion logic: TIME-OF-WETNESS kills galvanizing, not wetting frequency — vertical studs in a ventilated cavity dry in an hour; a sealed cavity that ever leaks stays humid for days. **Why the grill bay is the exception:** the under-grill zone must not have radiant line-of-sight through mesh to the already-scorched vinyl fence; back board pre-attached at tilt-up (a flat panel has a reachable back; a standing one never does again). **Why the kamado corner stays cloth (owner's call, correct):** ceramic kamados are thermoses — low exterior radiation — and the boarded, porcelain-faced backsplash wall already stands between body and fence.

### D15 — Grill bay: fully boarded compartment, SINGLE layer 🔒
**Superseded:** double ½" board + Rockwool (owner challenged: "who actually recommends this?" — nobody; deleted).
**Why single is correct:** steel + cement board is a non-combustible assembly (ASTM E136) — the entire requirement. Double layers and the "insulated jacket" solve combustible-island problems this build doesn't have. **Why boarded sides (both flanking partitions):** stops firebox heat + grease vapor migrating into adjacent storage (door backers are polyiso foam), defines the compartment so the manual's ventilation-area math means something (LP compartments have explicit vent rules — low openings, propane sinks), and stiffens the partitions carrying counter at the cutout. Bare board, taped, no membrane, no tile. Rockwool demoted to a 10-minute retrofit IF an adjacent bay ever runs warm.

### D16 — Track weep holes 🔒 (owner-originated)
**Why:** The bottom track is a U-channel gutter with no outlet — cavity water pools and standing water eats galvanizing. Fix: ¼" holes at the bottom of the BACK flange at the web corner, 1–2 per stud bay, every run, drilled on sawhorses. Works because the track's back flange slightly overhangs the PT (the FRONT flange is the flush one) → water exits sideways into the fence air gap onto open paver. Deburr + cold-galv every hole. Third instance of the "never give water a place to sit" principle.

### D17 — Base/finish geometry 🔒 (owner-corrected)
Track front flange flush with the PT FRONT face; stud web spans the full track interior. The finish stack (½" board + ~3/16" thinset + ~3/8" plank ≈ 1" proud of the stud face) drops PAST track and sill to a weep screed ~½" above the pavers — no wood visible at grade. Counter overhang, door reveals, and corner trim all reference the FINISHED face. See diagram-01.

### D18 — Drain: Option C, real pipe now, seated HIGH 🔒
**History:** full trench-to-sewer now → exposed fence run → stub-out (Option C) → matured into laying the actual first feet of drain.
**Why:** Plumbers assessed the house cleanout as shallow for the distance — the fall budget is TIGHT — so elevation is banked everywhere: 2" pipe (not 3": smaller pipe = higher invert = more fall to spend) seated directly beneath the pavers, trap arm exiting high (~18–20"). 2" ABS DWV, long-sweep 90 (never a vent elbow — the riser doubles as rodding access once the trap is pulled), riser capped 6–8" proud until trim-out, yard end capped at grade + photographed. Interim: sealed 5–7 gal jug with bulkhead fitting under the sink. Fallback ladder if the math fails: fence-run route (+~1 ft elevation) → utility drain pump (~$150–300, pressurized discharge runs uphill, problem ceases to exist). Tie-in math: fall available = trap height + cleanout invert depth; needed = run × ¼"/ft. Sewer tie-in should be permitted when it happens ⚠️.

### D19 — Gas: straight sleeve crossing + interior CSST routing 🔒
**Why a sleeve (not real pipe like the drain):** pressurized fuel gets installed, pressure-tested, and inspected as ONE operation by the installer; gravity wastewater doesn't. **Why straight is fine:** the future line is CSST, which bends by hand at the sleeve exit — rigid pipe needed the glued sweep, flexible pipe IS the bend. 18–24" of 2" PVC crossing only the seam, emerging beside the drain riser with vertical headroom. **Interior routing:** through the back wall's CENTERED stud-web knockouts — inside the wall plane, behind drawer travel by definition; centered in a 3-5/8" web = 1.5"+ from either face, beyond 1¼" screw reach → no strike plates on the knockout run (plates only where the line leaves center: the rise and the grill-bay turn). Grommets at every knockout regardless (abrasion ≠ strike). CSST gets bonded per manufacturer. **Corner chase:** 1¼–1½" PVC pre-installed through the kamado corner's knockouts during framing — once the cradle carries 400 lb of ceramic against the fence, that corner is blind forever. **Pre-run declined by default:** the island's interior stays accessible for life, so pre-running buys little and risks construction damage to untested line; if done anyway, Home-Flex (DIY-sold CSST), installer buy-in confirmed first, gauge-tee pressure test before and after.

### D20 — Third pipe: universal spare chase 🔒
**Why:** Empty conduit under a permanent structure is never wasted (future lighting, circuit, irrigation, water). The stated goal (hot water) has a better answer: a point-of-use heater at the sink (2.5-gal mini-tank, ~1440W/12A on the existing cold line + sink circuit, hot in seconds, zero trenching) — remember this probable appliance when weighing Phase 2 circuit loading. **Trench order across the seam: drain, spare, gas** — inches of sand between, nothing touching, gas OUTERMOST so no future dig at a water line crosses it.

### D21 — Electrical: two 20A circuits, conduit + THWN-2 🔒
Fridge dedicated (refrigeration hates sharing with a tripping GFCI); grill + kamado + sink convenience on the second. GFCI everything; weatherproof in-use covers. Conduit throughout (island interior = damp/wet location; no Romex/UF). Existing stubs relocated with PVC offset fittings BELOW track level, rising inside stud bays — never notch the cement board at grade. Rough-in happens DURING framing (before board closes faces); only device trim is late. Splices in an accessible j-box (fridge cavity hub).

### D22 — Curved kamado corner: alive 🔒
**System:** curved track (snip flanges every 1–2", bend web), studs ~6" o.c. on the arc, ¼" cement board in VERTICAL STRIPS one per bay (flat facets, zero bending stress — you cannot meaningfully bend cement board), galvanized expanded-metal lath wrapped continuously (the actual bendable non-combustible material; this is how pros curve masonry), mortar base coat screeded to true radius with a plywood template cut from the SketchUp arc. Finish: smooth stucco band as a deliberate accent framing the red kamado — planks stop at a trim line (they won't wrap the radius). Everything in the stack is non-combustible.

### D23 — Footprint & ergonomics 🔒
36" finished counter height; 31" counter depth (30" usable + rear overhang carrying the backsplash so it doesn't eat usable depth); island held 1.5–2" off the vinyl fence (heat + conduit + functional back vents); propane tank on pavers in the open under-grill bay with toe-kick venting and LP detector LOW.

### D24 — Waterproofing philosophy 🔒
Membrane (RedGard/AquaDefense) only where it earns its keep: counter deck + clad faces. Boarded back sections get NO membrane (they're the drying side). Z-flashing across the backsplash top, weep screed at cladding base, sloped cabinet floors to weep holes, sealed penetrations. UV clock: clad within 2–4 months of membrane application (UV degrades it; rain doesn't).

### D25 — Build order corrections 🔒
The owner's original 10-step order had two fixes: plumbing rough-in moved FIRST (can't trench under a built kitchen; underground work wants inspection before covering), and electrical rough-in moved from step 7 to DURING framing (boxes and conduit go through open studs; you can't fish a closed island). Countertop-before-backsplash was already correct (tile sits over the finished counter with a silicone joint).
